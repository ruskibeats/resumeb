### Psychological Success Profile — What winning looks like in each role

---

### ROLE A: INFRASTRUCTURE PROGRAMME DIRECTOR

**Core mandate psychology:** This role demands a leader who can hold complexity steady across time. The hiring panel is not looking for someone who works hard — they are looking for someone whose *cognitive operating system* is structured around programme-level thinking: dependencies, risk cascades, stakeholder confidence, and commercial cadence. The candidate who wins this role rarely needs to be the smartest person technically; they must be the most *systematically disciplined* person in the room.

**Behavioural competency profile (ranked by hiring weight):**

| Competency | Behavioural indicators expected at interview | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Ambiguity tolerance + structured response | Can narrate a complex, poorly-defined situation → how they imposed order → what framework/structure they introduced | Critical |
| Stakeholder confidence management | Evidence of board/SteerCo-level communication; can name who they reported to, at what cadence, and what they reported | Critical |
| Decision-making under incomplete information | Does not defer endlessly; can describe making a call with 70% information and standing by it | Critical |
| Commercial programme ownership | Owns budget variance conversations; knows the commercial levers, not just the delivery ones | High |
| Risk escalation discipline | Surfaces issues early and appropriately; does not absorb risk silently | High |
| Multi-vendor orchestration | Holds multiple third parties to account simultaneously; has direct examples of vendor challenge/renegotiation | High |
| Recovery mindset | Has a "we were behind, here's what I did" story — not just "I delivered on time" | Medium |
| Resilience signalling | Appears steady in interview; gives grounded, non-defensive answers to challenge questions | Medium |

**Decision style profile (occupational psychology framing):**
- **Preferred decision style:** Evidence-led with structured option framing. Presents options to executive sponsors rather than seeking endorsement of pre-made decisions.
- **Risk tolerance profile:** Medium-high. Comfortable committing to delivery positions under uncertainty when governance mechanisms are in place; avoids reckless confidence.
- **Cognitive operating mode:** Long-range dependency thinker — naturally tracks "what breaks if this slips" two or three steps ahead.
- **Stress signature:** Likely responds to programme pressure with increased structure (more governance, more check-ins, sharper reporting) rather than with avoidance or escalation-as-relief.

**Leadership archetype for this role:** The **Steady Accountable** — calm authority, evidence-first communication, visible when things go wrong. Not the inspirational visionary; the executive who holds programme confidence through turbulence.

**Stakeholder influence pattern:**
- Gains trust through *consistency of information* — same facts, framed clearly, at every board
- Builds credibility with technical teams by demonstrating they understand enough to challenge without micromanaging
- Manages upward by reducing uncertainty, not by promising certainty
- Manages vendors through contractual and governance clarity — not through relationship-reliance alone

**Execution traits panel is screening for:**
- Can mobilise a programme from scratch (team, governance, delivery plan)
- Runs RAID discipline — not just as a document but as a live risk-reduction mechanism
- Closes issues — not just flags them
- Maintains delivery rhythm through personnel changes or vendor failures

**What disqualifies candidates at interview:**
- Cannot name programme budget, milestone structure, or governance cadence from their own examples
- Gives vague answers to recovery/risk questions ("we worked together to resolve it")
- Defensiveness when probed on failure moments
- Technical knowledge presented instead of delivery authority — tells the interviewer *what* the technology was rather than *what they decided* about it

### ROLE B: SENIOR INFRASTRUCTURE PROGRAMME MANAGER

**Core mandate psychology:** One level below Programme Director, but the psychological difference is meaningful. The Senior PM is the person who *makes the plan happen* — not who sets the strategic direction, but who converts decisions into executable workstream plans and holds cross-functional teams to them. The winning candidate is disciplined, organised, and politically intelligent within the programme — they know when to escalate and when to absorb. They are the backbone of delivery confidence, not its figurehead.

**Behavioural competency profile (ranked by hiring weight):**

| Competency | Behavioural indicators expected at interview | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Dependency and schedule management | Can describe a complex dependency map and how they managed changes to it in real-time | Critical |
| Cross-functional coordination | Evidence of managing stakeholders across Product, Engineering, Operations, Commercial simultaneously — without formal authority over all | Critical |
| Risk identification and early escalation | Specific examples of surfacing risk before it became a crisis | Critical |
| Plan integrity discipline | Has maintained or recovered a programme plan under pressure; can narrate exactly how | High |
| Stakeholder communication (C-1 level) | Confident briefing programme sponsors or directors; not necessarily board-level | High |
| Workstream performance management | Experience managing PM/workstream-lead performance within a programme | High |
| Delivery cadence ownership | Runs weekly/fortnightly delivery forums; tracks actions to closure; doesn't let things drift | High |
| Analytical rigour | Produces reporting that is accurate, concise, and decision-enabling (not vanity reporting) | Medium |

**Decision style profile:**
- **Preferred decision style:** Data-first, protocol-respectful. More likely to seek approval than to act unilaterally at boundaries. When operating within clear mandate, highly decisive.
- **Risk tolerance:** Medium. Disciplined risk-taker within defined parameters; escalates genuinely ambiguous risk rather than absorbing it.
- **Cognitive operating mode:** Tracker/resolver — holds multiple streams simultaneously; notices when one drifts before the Programme Director does.
- **Stress signature:** Likely increases analytical activity under pressure (more check-ins, more detailed tracking). May resist pushing back on scope addition if authority lines aren't explicit.

**Leadership archetype for this role:** The **Delivery Engine** — makes things happen without drama; respected by technical leads and trusted by programme directors; earns influence through reliability, not seniority.

**Stakeholder influence pattern:**
- Earns credibility through accuracy (what they say will happen, happens)
- Gains technical team trust by understanding enough to have an informed conversation
- Manages upward by producing reporting that requires no editing
- Manages peers by being the most organised person in the room

**Execution traits panel is screening for:**
- Owns and maintains a detailed programme plan
- Runs cross-workstream forums with discipline (action-focused, time-boxed)
- Produces accurate RAID, milestone tracking, and progress reports
- Manages change control rigorously — doesn't let scope drift go undocumented

**What disqualifies candidates at interview:**
- Cannot describe a specific dependency that changed mid-programme and what they did
- Reporting experience is vague — "I updated the project plan" without substance
- No evidence of managing peers or matrix teams without formal authority
- Presents as strategy-focused — panels will sense a mismatch with execution demands

### ROLE C: TECHNICAL OPERATIONS DIRECTOR (INTERIM)

**Core mandate psychology:** This is the most operationally intense of the three roles psychologically. Unlike the Programme Director (who leads a time-limited programme), the interim Technical Operations Director is accountable for *keeping everything running* while also changing it. The psychological demand is dual-track cognition: steady operational vigilance AND strategic reset capability. The winning candidate is not merely calm — they are *calmly purposeful*, with a clear internal model of what good operations looks like and the authority to redirect the organisation toward it.

**Behavioural competency profile (ranked by hiring weight):**

| Competency | Behavioural indicators expected at interview | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| BAU operational authority | Has owned service delivery, SLA performance, and escalation governance — not just programme delivery | Critical |
| Commercial and P&L instinct | Talks about operations in terms of cost-to-serve, margin, contract economics — not just delivery quality | Critical |
| Vendor governance and accountability | Has had difficult vendor conversations; has invoked SLA remedies or threatened/executed exit | Critical |
| Rapid diagnostic capability | Can assess an operational environment quickly; knows what the early warning signs of dysfunction look like | Critical |
| Executive presence under pressure | Has communicated bad operational news to boards or clients; didn't hide it | High |
| People leadership at operational scale | Has built, reset, or restructured an operational team; not just managed an inherited one | High |
| Process and governance design | Has designed operational rhythms, not just operated within existing ones | High |
| Interim mandate discipline | Understands the specific psychology of interim work: land fast, establish trust, deliver visible change, hand over cleanly | High |

**Decision style profile:**
- **Preferred decision style:** Direct and swift. In an interim context, time is compressed — the winning candidate makes confident operational calls quickly, backs them with evidence, and moves.
- **Risk tolerance:** High within operational mandate; medium when changes touch commercial or strategic territory (knows when to involve principals).
- **Cognitive operating mode:** Pattern-recognition-first — scans for structural dysfunction signals across SLAs, team behaviour, vendor relationships, and cost data simultaneously.
- **Stress signature:** Calm under operational pressure — likely becomes more directive under stress, not more consultative. Will intervene directly when the situation demands it (field intervention model).

**Leadership archetype for this role:** The **Operational Authority** — arrives with credibility, diagnoses quickly, acts decisively, builds trust through visible operational improvement. Combines personal technical credibility with commercial accountability.

**Stakeholder influence pattern:**
- Gains trust with the board by presenting clean, fact-based operational intelligence — no spin
- Gains trust with the team by demonstrating they understand the work and won't sacrifice delivery quality for optics
- Manages vendors with explicit commercial leverage — performance frameworks, contractual remedies
- Manages clients through transparency and escalation control

**Execution traits panel is screening for:**
- Has designed or redesigned an operating model (not inherited it passively)
- Owns P&L or cost-to-serve accountability — not just delivery
- Has led an operational team through change or instability
- Can describe a specific service crisis and how they led the response

**What disqualifies candidates at interview:**
- No BAU service delivery ownership — only programme/project delivery
- Cannot quantify operational outcomes (SLA attainment %, cost saved, team size)
- Defers to the team when asked what *they personally* decided in a pressure scenario
- Presents as a strategy/advisory type — panels will push for execution ownership and must see it clearly

### Gap 1: Conflict and Pushback Evidence — CRITICAL

**Nature of gap:** Across two interview phases, Russell deferred on direct questions about conflict, pushback, and disagreement with senior stakeholders. No concrete "I told the board they were wrong" story has been extracted.

**Why it matters:** Senior panel interviews for Programme Director and Operations Director roles consistently include at least one probe in this area. Panellists are looking for emotional regulation under challenge — not aggression, but the willingness to hold a position when pushed. A candidate who cannot produce this story appears either accommodating to the point of risk, or evasive about a weakness.

**Psychological interpretation:** The pattern of deferral may reflect one of: (a) genuine conflict avoidance as a preference, (b) preference for resolving through clarity rather than confrontation, (c) high self-awareness about how conflict stories can go wrong in interview. Option (b) is most consistent with the evidence pattern and is actually a valid style — but it needs to be named and evidenced, not absent.

**Mitigation — coaching:**
1. Reframe the NATO recovery story to include the specific moment of challenge: who objected to the restructure, what was said, and how Russell held the position. This likely happened and simply hasn't been articulated.
2. Identify one example from the PE-backed engagements where Russell disagreed with a commercial or operational decision made above him — what happened next.
3. Prepare a three-sentence answer to: *"Tell me about a time you told a board or CEO something they didn't want to hear."* Structure: situation → my position → what I said → what happened.

**Target:** One clear, well-rehearsed board-level disagreement narrative. Does not need to be adversarial — mature, evidence-led pushback is the most credible form.

**Q8 (Panel version):**
*"You join an operation that is demoralised — previous leader left a mess, client is hostile. What do you do in week one, and what are you looking for?"*

**Why this is asked:** Tests diagnostic style, leadership in adverse conditions, and the ability to sequence action correctly (listen before changing).

**Evidence anchor:** General operating style — pattern recognition, calm under pressure, cadence restoration.

Week one is about establishing credibility, not making structural changes.
